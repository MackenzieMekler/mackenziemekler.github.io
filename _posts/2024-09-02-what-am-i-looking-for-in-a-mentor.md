As I quickly approach the start of my first rotation, I feel bombarded by the question: “What are you looking for in your mentor?”. I have been asked this by my friends, family, professors, and worst of all myself. The truth is that this represents the first time that I really have control over the type of mentoring that I will receive and I am not sure how to answer it. But, that being said, I will soon need to have an idea because in the next month I will be in a lab with one rotation slot filled and only two left to choose my home lab and PhD advisor. 

In order to discover what I am looking for in a mentor, I have decided to reflect on some of my past lab mentors and experiences and how they positively and negatively impacted me. 

### The Brusko Lab

The first time that I was able to experience mentorship in research was in my first lab at the University of Florida. In the Brusko lab I had two mentors of sorts, one was Dr. Brusko and the other was a graduate student named Martin. Dr. Brusko took a very hands-off approach with his students. I noticed that this is something many of the older graduate students seemed to appreciate however, many of the undergraduates and newer students wished to have more active mentoring to help them learn. Luckily this role was filled by our lab techs, research assistants, and for me by Martin. 

Martin was a very good first research mentor and he taught me most of what I needed to know including basic cell culture and aseptic technique. He had a very good balance of being hands-on at first and then quickly tapering off once he was confident that I had learned a technique. He was also always willing to answer my questions about the basic science behind what we were doing. 

It was this experience that taught me that I appreciate an environment where people will help me to learn new techniques and answer questions but once I am more established I do enjoy a more hands off approach. 

### The Bonning Lab

Very quickly after working in the Brusko lab, I transitioned into working in the Bonning lab under a Dr. Ke Wu. Dr. Wu was very talented in cell culture and was meticulous in his aseptic technique. Dr. Wu was a very hands-on teacher and helped me to improve my cell culture techniques greatly. 

Something that I appreciated greatly about Dr. Wu is that when we encountered problems in our experiments he actively asked for and weighed my input. I think this openness to listen to my ideas helped me to grow as a researcher immensely and made me feel that I was a greater part of the work and the lab. This quickly became a quality that I greatly valued in a mentor. 

The work I conducted at the Bonning Lab was very short however, I am very grateful to have been a part of this kind of scientific discourse to learn how scientists and by extension my future mentors should discuss with me. 

### The Swanson Lab

The final lab that I was fortunate enough to be a part of was the Swanson Lab. It was in this lab that I had my two most influential mentors. The mentor I worked directly under was a graduate student named Ben. Ben was an incredible teacher who combined some of the best parts of my last two mentors. Ben was very patient in teaching and explaining new techniques in the lab but once I learned them, he became more hands-off and trusted me to do them on my own. Additionally, once I became more comfortable with our work, Ben asked for and took my input on our experiments and papers. 

One of the new traits I found in Ben as a mentor was his ability to hold me accountable. In one particular instance, I had mixed up deadlines and needed him to help me compile a paper in just 3 days. Rather than being understandably upset with me and not addressing it or not helping which would have been considerably easier, Ben took the time to take me aside and explain to me how what I did was unprofessional and how it affected him. He managed to do this patiently and empathetically which helped me understand better my mistake and grow from the experience without just feeling bad about myself as a person. This balance of patient and empathetic but with rigid accountability is incredibly difficult and something I have learned to value highly in a mentor. 

The last large thing I learned in this lab is something that Ben and Dr. Swanson both shared. This was the importance of having a mentor that challenges you. When I asked Ben questions about science or when background concepts would arise, he would respond often with different questions to guide me to a final answer rather than just providing it. Dr. Swanson additionally did this both in lab meetings and in feedback on papers. I found that this was incredibly helpful for learning in the lab as well as for preparing for presentations. Having a mentor who will ask you your hardest questions and help you learn from them has helped me grow so much as a scientist and researcher.  

On top of this, Dr. Swanson managed to show me how important a lab environment can be. In my other two labs, most researchers did their own things and there was not a large sense of community. However, in the Swanson lab, most people would talk with each other about projects as well as their lives outside of the lab. This helped me feel so connected with the other people in lab with me. This led to me getting help on my applications or presentations from so many researchers in the lab both undergraduate and post-graduate. Dr. Swanson helped greatly to build this environment by coming into lab to talk with people regularly (even his undergraduates) and holding celebrations such as graduation lunches and birthday celebrations. 

### Orientation Advice

Looking through my labs helped me so much to gain a starting point for some qualities that were important for me in a mentor but there was so much else to look for as well. During my orientation week, Emory held a graduate student class called JPE600 where I was able to meet in a discussion group and hear some opinions on mentorship that my fellow graduate students had. At the beginning we were asked to list the most important trait that we were looking for. I have compiled a word cloud from the answers that were given. 

![wordcloud.png](/assets/graphics/2024-09-02-what-am-i-looking-for-in-a-mentor/wordcloud.png "Mentorship Word Cloud")

As you can see, many people touched on points that I had already seen during my undergraduate time. The three most common were trust, availability, and empathy. These were three things I had already learned were important to me. However there were others that I had not considered as deeply. For example, communication is incredibly important. This was mentioned in multiple different contexts throughout our discussion. The least obvious to me was in the context of expectations both from your advisor to you and vis versa. Many of my classmates stated that they wanted to work with an advisor that would clearly set expectations for them that way they could know what they needed to do or work on. For me this makes a lot of sense because I am still a very new researcher and thus do not always know if I am doing something wrong or not doing something I should be. Clear communication from an advisor would help me fix this. This is also important for rotations because it helps you know what will be expected of you should you choose that lab (late nights, weekends, conferences, etc.). The thing that was particularly interesting to me was that some people added that advisors should also be able to accept your communication about what you need out of an advisor. By making this a two way street, all of the benefits you can provide your mentor from knowing what they want, they can also provide to you. This is something I hope to find in a future mentor as I have learned that it can make so many parts of your PhD easier. 

### The Business Side of Things

Most of the traits that I have mentioned so far have been personal qualities in a mentor. At least personal in such that it relates to how your mentor relates to other people, communicates, and acts on a relationship level or in my words, a more personal level. These are all incredibly important and have been mentioned first because as an undergraduate it is all that I had to worry about and all that greatly affected me and my classmates. As a PhD student and beyond, professional qualities in a PI can have a profound effect on your livelihood as well. 

These are qualities that I have begun to learn about through the JPE course I had already mentioned as well as through talking to students, faculty, and attending other orientation events. The issue that I am facing with these qualities is that they are not very black and white. Most of these are not inherently good or bad but need to be weighed out for each individual person to find the appropriate match.

For example, how well-known a professor is in their field would be a professional aspect of their lab. More well-known professors come with a lot of benefits such as improved networking, helping your grants and publications be better received, and being able to help you move into a career after graduation. This last one is especially important. PIs are one of the easiest ways to be connected with a post-doc or job after graduating so it is nice to have one that has connections that could help you. Additionally, it is good to have one that will work with you regardless of your career aspirations rather than trying to push you in one direction or the other. Regardless, being well known comes with some downsides. More well-known professors tend to be busier which they may demand from their students as well. Additionally their availability may suffer because of having many different responsibilities. 

Another factor that I am currently trying to balance is how frequently a PI publishes papers. Publishing papers is enormously important for building CVs, graduating from schools, receiving funding, etc.. Because of this, quickly publishing papers is important to a grad student. There is a drawback with publishing quickly though. Publishing in high impact (often better) journals requires more time and is also very important to a CV. Moreso, with more frequent publication comes more pressure to get results and do experiments that are a “sure thing”. 

Something I had not considered is that how new a professor is to a university would be one of these qualities to look at. When a professor is new, they often have a higher pressure to publish papers which ties into the quality above. Additionally, for those interested in academia (of which I am one) being with a new professor allows you to see someone build up their lab at a university which is something you will have to do. The downside of this is that you kind of roll the dice with a new lab. Funding can be less stable because they do not have a history of getting grants. Networking/collaboration can also be more difficult as your PI does not have their own networks established.

The last big example of these traits that I am struggling with is the work-life balance of my professor and more importantly, the work-life balance they expect of their students. Graduate school is hard and research is not always the most convenient profession. I am aware of this and that with that comes the need to work weekends or late nights occasionally. My favorite quote about this is that in academia you will work 18 hours of the day but you get to choose which 18 hours. There are many PIs in the world and many with different philosophies on when their students should be in the lab. My problem is that my life outside of the lab still matters to me for my mental and physical health as well as just for who I am. While I am willing to conform to the demand of academia, I will need this to not come at the complete sacrifice of my life. 

### In Conclusion

I would love to be able to give a beautiful and artful conclusion to this post outlining exactly how important each one of these qualities is to me and the perfect balance that I have achieved with them all. In this heavenly (and imaginary) world I would end this post by revealing that I had found Dr. Blahblahblah at Emory who is looking for students and perfectly matched all of my criteria. Unfortunately that is not how this worked out. The truth is that I do not know the perfect combination of these traits. Furthermore, even if I did, this isn’t “Weird Science” and I can’t just plug those into a computer and make a PI that perfectly follows all of them. 

Currently I am just trying to find all of these traits that could be important. By talking about them here I have helped myself get more familiar with them and have put them on paper now rather than leaving them bouncing around my head. My current plan is to evaluate the people that I meet based on these traits I have listed. To show where they fall on the spectrum that each trait is and hopefully just find a few that I think I could work well with based on that layout. 

This post is coming in a kind of part 1 and part 2 fashion. After this one (possibly in a month or so) I hope to make a second that will be centered on how I picked the professors I will be rotating with. This post will demonstrate (hopefully well) how I used these “metrics” to organize some of the people I meet and the decisions I make when deciding which lab to pursue among other parts of solidifying rotations. 

### Final Note

After editing this post I noticed that I have kind of glaringly left out the work that a PI’s lab does as one of my metrics. This is indeed an important metric (more for some and less for others) and should be considered when picking people to rotate with. Most people say that to select a good lab you need to have enough interest in the work that you could see yourself doing it for upwards of 6 years. 

There was a few reasons that this was not included however. 

The first reason is I’m writing primarily for myself (selfish, I know) and I am not entirely sure what work I do and don’t want to do. I am very interested in immunology but I also enjoy looking at microenvironment, genetics, combination therapies, and more. Because of this, I am trying to not let this factor guide my decision too much. 

The second reason is that I have found that different people view this with wildly different levels of importance. While this is kind of true with most traits, I have found it is most extreme with this one. There are some people in my program who do not care at all what work someone is doing as long as they feel that they can work well with them. On the other hand I have met people that would work with the Devil himself as long as he was researching a specific topic. As with the other traits it is important to weigh this out for yourself. 

If you are in the same position as me, I wish you the best of luck on your journey and I hope that you find a faculty member that will help turn you into whatever kind of scientist you hope to be!
