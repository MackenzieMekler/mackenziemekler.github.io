Academic research experiences are a cornerstone of undergraduate achievement in the sciences. Most undergraduates interested in the sciences aim to participate in research of some kind, especially those who are more interested in a PhD than medical school. And there is good reason for this. Undergraduate research is valuable for so many reasons such as :

- Resumes
- Real experience in scientific research (pre-PhD mainly)
- Demonstrating a deeper understanding of science
- Learning a lot about a specific field of science

However, research positions can be really hard to find. This is something that I have experienced as well as many of my friends in STEM. Because of this mix of desire and lack of availability, I decided to make a post about what I think are the most effective ways to get involved in research. 

### Emails, Emails, Emails

The most common and least fun way to find research positions is to email faculty. In many cases, this can be a necessary approach but it can be difficult because the vast majority of the emails will not return a position. For this reason, numbers are incredibly important. Many undergraduates will send upwards of 20 emails at a time trying to find faculty with open research positions. In my experience the breakdown that you can expect is that 90% of PIs won’t respond, 5% will say that they do not have an open position, and 5% will say that they would like to meet you and talk more. And it is important to mention that this is a relatively optimistic breakdown.

In my opinion, cold emailing is the least effective of my methods for finding research but its benefit is that it requires nothing and is something that you can always do to possibly find a position. 

### Networking

This was the most successful way that I found my research experiences and in my opinion, is one of the easiest. The only thing that is required to start networking in research as an undergraduate is the ability to talk with new people and a genuine interest in research or science. I got my positions at the Brusko Lab, Swanson Lab, and my interviews in the Neurosurgery department all through networking with people in my classes. That is the most amazing part, I wasn’t even talking to professors or PIs, I was talking largely to my classmates and students older than me. Many undergraduates are involved in research and when you talk to them you can find out how they got their experience, what it is they do, and in good situations, they might even tell you that they have a spot available. 

It is also possible to do this with professors in your classes because most of your professors do research. Using professors is a good way to get around cold emailing because if they recognize your name from their class, you are much more likely to get a response. There are a few suggestions that I have for how to approach professors: 

1. Research them beforehand 
    1. Find their profile in your university’s website and read through a few of their papers. It will be hard to convince them you are interested in their research if you don’t even know what they do. Plus, you will stand out if you know specific things about their recent research papers because it will show that you are willing to put in the work and do readings. 
2. Start your conversations as an interested party do not just directly ask for a position
    1. Remember the most helpful thing to networking is genuine interest so it is always good to start here. Ask what their future directions are, what techniques they use, what the big picture goal of their research is, and maybe even why they chose the field that they are in. 
3. After a few meetings or a little while ask them if there are any open positions for an undergraduate or what the minimum requirements are. 
    1. Just because you got to know them does not mean that they have space for an undergraduate researcher. It is best to ask and just hope for the best. Additionally, some PIs will have minimum class requirements depending on their research field so that undergraduates will understand what is happening. This question is good to ask because even if they don’t have space or you need to take a class first, you will have made a connection and can email to check back in in the future. Because of this connection, your email would be much more likely to get a response. 

### Email Chains, Groups

This is the last way that I have found to find research positions is through group threads and email chains. Many university departments will have email chains that students can join where the department will send notices for internship positions, club meetings, and open research positions. It was a thread that gave me the opportunity that I found from the Bonning lab. These are pretty self-explanatory and the hardest part about them is finding and being added to the chain. For every university this is different but asking advisors and classmates is a good place to start.
