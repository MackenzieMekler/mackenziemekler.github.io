I have the first exam for my cancer biology PhD program in 5 days and counting. 

This exam has been kind of intimidating. Staying true to form I have been talking to the older PhD students in the program and have gotten very mixed feedback. One frightening thing to know is that in the previous years, students tend to do really poorly on the first exam especially. However, I have gotten a wide spread of feedback on how I can hopefully avoid this. 

Our exam will be 3 questions and we will have up to 4 hours to complete it. Each question will consist of multiple parts and will be written by the professor who taught us that material. This creates the first major hurdle that I have been trying to jump: What material am I supposed to know? 

In my undergraduate there were very clear guidelines of what I should know and what I needed to study. We had learning objectives, study guides, and while we did apply our knowledge there was a large component of our grades that was based on memorization. This is very different now. Our lecturers gave of broad overviews of concepts but are far more interested in how we can apply both what they have taught and our background knowledge to solve problems. While this is a far more practical way to test, it has also made the process of studying more difficult. 

This is where my first piece of advice has come in. Our DGS (director of graduate studies) held a meeting to talk to us about this first exam and gave us a crucial piece of wisdom. Read, read, read. 

A PhD and a career in science is a lot of reading and frankly it needs to be. It is not possible to be an effective researcher if you fall behind on the science of your field. Reading papers will give you a more wholistic understanding of the material and will provide the methods and experiments that real researchers do in order to test these characteristics. Seeing that most of our test questions will be application/experimentally based, knowing these methods is incredibly practical (and more broadly practical to our lives as researchers). 

I am currently trying to incorporate this into my studying in two main ways. Firstly I am reading review papers on what has been discussed in class, especially topics that are talked about a lot and/or that I am not familiar with. For example, being our first exam we have talked a lot about carcinogenesis and the hallmarks of cancer so I am slowly getting through the three editions of the “Hallmarks of Cancer” review papers written by Hanahan and Weinberg. The other way is by reading any papers that are mentioned in lecture. One of our lecturers talked about two papers that used different model systems to study oncogenes and I will be reading both of those soon in order to see how these systems can be used and what kinds of experiments can be done in them for our topic. 

I am still at the beginning stages of implementing this advice but I can already say that it has made me feel more confident. Reading science can be difficult but I have already learned more methods than I would otherwise know and it has started to make me more comfortable with reading figures and understanding experimental design which will both be crucial moving forwards. 

The second piece of advice that I am still experimenting with and deciding how to approach is to learn and understand molecular pathways. For our first exam it seems I will need to know two broad kinds of pathways: those linked with the hallmarks and DNA damage repair pathways. 

From what I have heard, it is not often practical to memorize entire pathways and it is not expected of you. The important things to know are the first protein (that gets the signal) and the last protein (the effector that the pathway modulates). Then from here it is useful to know the kinds of proteins involved in this (kinase, endonuclease, etc.) and have a general idea of how the signal traverses the cell. For this I think my current approach will be to see and understand the pathways that I am supposed to know and then to see how they are investigated experimentally through research papers. 

The final hint I have been given about our exams is that I should understand how to design experiments because I will likely be asked to. In general, experimental design should follow an outline:

1. What are you aiming to measure?
2. How will this be measured?
3. What readout would this experiment give?
4. What is the expected data and how does this apply to your hypothesis?
    1. What if you were wrong? Do you have alternative hypotheses?

There are of course other aspects to this answer including controls and shortcomings that need to be acknowledged in certain methods. My plan currently that I have gotten to use a little is to practice this by trying to design alternative experiments in papers to what the authors did or to think of how to test some of their future questions. This will give me some practice of walking through these questions and steps. 

This first exam is admittedly stressful but I have been fortunate to be a part of a program that is aimed towards my success and to be surrounded by classmates that I can study and work with. I have a long 5 days ahead of me for my first exam but I am excited to talk about this again with all of you on the 6th to see what I will keep and change for my second.
