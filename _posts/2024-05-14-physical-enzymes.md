The average biologist learns about the electron transport chain (ETC) 4 times in their life by my estimate. The first is in middle school where this intricate process is not even given a name. It is simply grouped together as we learn that “the mitochondrion is the powerhouse of the cell.” The second happens in high school when this energy generation is listed in its three principal steps: glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, and the ETC. It was the third and fourth time that I learned this chain that it changed the way I viewed biochemistry. 

ATP synthase was the reason for this. In a grossly simplified overview, the ETC exists to use energy from NADP and FADH2 to move hydrogen ions across a membrane. By allowing hydrogens back across the membrane but only through the ATP synthase, a water mill of sorts is created but this mill powers almost every single eukaryotic cell.

This protein rotates very much in the same way generators do to produce the same product… Energy! It was this realization that changed the way that I viewed biochemistry. Chemistry is often thought of so separately from physics and the laws that govern our “macro” world but they are not different. ATP synthase harnesses energy from a gradient and motion to catalyze the formation of ATP in the same way that a water wheel harnesses the flow of water from high to low to produce electricity. 

I titled this post Physical Enzymes because while I know all enzymes are physical objects, too many students forget this (myself included). It is too easy to sign off an enzyme as a mystical chemical that makes a reaction happen and forget that it is governed by physical forces. Not only does this give an appreciation for the adaptability of nature and its willingness to use any forces or measures necessary to complete a goal, but it yields practical use as well. Many drugs work by enzyme inhibition and some of the most common of these block an enzyme sterically, or in other words, by getting in the way of the other objects interacting with the enzyme. 

Even in organic chemistry reactions, steric hindrance can slow or even stop a reaction. This is easily seen in Sn2 reactions which lie a little out of this scope but are interesting and very useful in organic synthesis.

I started this conversation with ATP synthase because it was truly my first experience with this concept but it is not necessarily the easiest to visualize. In fact a much easier example may be one of the “cutest” proteins you will ever see.

![Walking Protein](/assets/graphics/2024-05-14-physical-enzymes/gif3.gif "Walking Protein")
{: .w-50 .rounded-10 .right}

Cell motor proteins utilize physical forces to fulfill their tasks in walking materials from one place in a cell to another. I think it is important to clarify here that walking was a carefully selected word. These cells do not simply transport materials but they quite literally walk along the cytoskeleton using molecular feet to transport materials. 

These little proteins remind me of ancient Greek *hemerodromes* or messengers who would carry notes and papers between the high-ranking officials of different Greek states. These couriers were known for their fast running speeds of up to 6.2 miles per hour sustained over incredible distances. These motor proteins are no less dedicated. Myosin which is a type of transport protein is able to move at 0.2-60 micrometers per second which if adjusted to the size of a human being would equate to about 147 miles per hour. These proteins do this every second of every day to transport important signals across a cell. 

Once again, chemical reactions are not responsible for this movement. It is the physical walking of these proteins that transports important vesicles at remarkable speeds. And just like when we walk, there are complications to this including the size of the package and the drag caused by cytoplasm.

The laws of physics have final rule over this world. In day-to-day life, this is easy to remember. This is why we don’t get up from bed and fly into the ceiling and it is why I am able to spend my free time enthralled in volleyball, soccer, tennis, or a different sport. However, it is easy to forget that the domain of these forces extends within the cell as well. I have only given two examples in this post from the generators in our mitochondria to the *heterodromes* transporting vesicles but physical forces dominate so much of cell biology and biochemistry and I hope that this has opened you up to recognizing them.
