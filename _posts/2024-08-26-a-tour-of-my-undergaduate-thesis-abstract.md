The abstract is the first and sometimes only part of your research paper that will get read. It is usually under 250 words long and its main purpose is to communicate the essence of your paper quickly so a reader can know if they want to read the rest of it. After this, the abstract serves as a template for your paper that prepares a reader to follow its story and can serve as a quick reference to remember the most essential points made in your paper. 

In order to serve this purpose an abstract has section of sorts. Most often these “sections” are not actually separated in the abstract but depending on the guidelines of the journal you are submitting to this may not be the case. In an abstract the sections are as follows:

1. Background/Context
    1. Should include research question and gap
2. Methods 
3. Results 
4. Conclusions

This outline of an abstract was called an hourglass by one of my sources I read in preparation for this which is a very good visual analogy. Scientific papers start off very broad when giving a background and slowly narrow down into the specific gap/question/topic of interest. This is the narrowing from big concept down to narrow research paper. Then after the results are shown, the discussion and conclusions take the small answered questions and begin to expand into conclusions, implications, and further research questions for the field as a whole. This creates are narrowing and then widening that looks like and hourglass. As abstracts are just the research paper condensed into a few sentences, they also should follow this hourglass-like shape. 

### Introduction

> Myotonic dystrophy (DM) is the most common adult-onset muscular dystrophy. This is a genetically inherited disorder caused by CTG repeat expansions in the *DMPK* gene. These CTG repeats are then transcribed into toxic repetitive RNAs that sequester muscleblind-like (MBNL) splicing factors. This sequestration causes a loss of effective MBNL proteins leading to a shift in the transcriptome from adult to fetal RNA splicing patterns. This is a multisystemic disease where the muscle tissue has predominantly been studied while central nervous system (CNS) pathology is understudied. DM1 patients have many neurological symptoms including insomnia, excessive daytime sleepiness, memory deficits, executive dysfunction and early onset of cerebral atrophy. In this study, we examined how neural stem cells (NSCs) may be impacted in disease in order to further understand the cause of these neurological symptoms.
> 

Above I have copied the first few sentences of my abstract which constitute my introduction. We can already see from this how my abstract starts very broad (DM as a whole) and then slowly narrows by talking about the pathomechanism, the specific organ system of interest, and then the protein and cell type within that organ that we are studying in our paper. While doing this we efficiently established the research gap that we intend to fill as well shown by:

> This is a multisystemic disease where the muscle tissue has predominantly been studied while central nervous system (CNS) pathology is understudied.
> 

and our research question: 

> In this study, we examined how neural stem cells (NSCs) may be impacted in disease in order to further understand the cause of these neurological symptoms.
> 

One of the largest pieces of feedback that I encountered when writing my abstract introduction was to stay on topic. DM is a multisystemic disease and I had a tendency to talk about the muscle pathology (the predominantly studied area) as well as going far too deep into the cellular pathomechanism of disease. These take up words and ultimately distract from the key ideas of the paper. As I was not confirming the cellular biology of this disease or studying the muscle, these were not the main focus of my paper, they did not belong in my abstract. 

### Methods

This is one section that I did not include while writing my abstract. At the time I did not think that methods belonged in an abstract and my PI agreed. This has taught me to some extent that inclusion of this section is partially based on the preference of your PI and where you are submitting the paper. As I have no experience writing this section, I will not go into any depth with it; however, this is something I hope to change in the future and will likely update. 

### Results/Conclusions

> We find that MBNL1 and MBNL2 are both expressed in the nucleus of NSCs. Additionally, we demonstrate that loss of MBNL1/2 impacts formation of neurospheres in culture while not impacting cell proliferation. Finally, preliminary data suggests loss of MBNL proteins impacts differentiation of NSCs. In summary, we propose that NSCs may be impaired in DM, which could explain a subset of neurological symptoms seen in patients.
> 

Above are the results that I chose to present in my abstract. These three basic results encapsulate the majority of what my paper had discovered. After my presented results, the last sentence of my paper encapsulates the main discussion and conclusion point: “NSCs may be impaired” (analysis of data) and this “could explain a subset of… symptoms” (conclusion from analysis). 

This segment of my abstract is relatively small compared to the introduction. From my experience reading papers, this can happen or not largely just depending on the project and the preference of the writer. In my case, my thesis was centered on gathering preliminary data for something that would turn into a full paper at my lab. Thus, I did not end with a lot of data and most of it was not incredibly significant data. This was unfortunate but a pretty common part of writing an undergraduate thesis. 

The part of this section that I think is worth noting the most is that it prepares the reader well to understand the results and discussion sections of my paper. These results are presented in an important order: 1) protein of interest is in place of interest, 2) proliferative behavior unaffected, 3) differentiation affected. Together (and with some more analysis) these lead to the preliminary conclusion that this cell type may be impaired which could logically contribute to observed symptoms talked about in the introduction. 

### Closing Thoughts

I am an inexperienced scientific writer. This is a large reason for me writing posts like this. I am hopeful that by analyzing my writing and feedback I will be able to improve and possibly help a few others on the way. 

One of the reasons that I did not talk very much about feedback on this abstract is that I wrote this with my mentor in preparation for a presentation and thus did not receive much feedback when it was copied into the research paper. Because of this, I have chosen to focus on advice when writing abstracts and how the two of us incorporated this into the design of this abstract. 

Abstract design and writing is a difficult skill. It requires the pinnacle of concision and the ability to discern what the important points you have made are. This is one of the skills I would like to most refine as a PhD student because it is essential to publishing papers, speaking at conferences, winning certain awards/distinctions, and grant funding. Additionally, once a paper has been published, a well-written abstract can help generate citations.
