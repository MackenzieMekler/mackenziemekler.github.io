The Very Beginning

So many times I have been asked "When did you 'get into' science?". There are moments in my life where I felt my love for the sciences grow. One that I frequently use to answer this in applications or conversation is stepping into my first lab in college. While this was a transformative experience for me the real moment is slightly more embarassing.

In my elementary school there was a pride award given to the student that performed the highest in a certain exam for a specific subject. My 5th grade self wanted nothing more in the world than to win the math pride award and be the best mathematician in the 5th grade. However, when the time came, I lost the math award but had somehow won the science? I at this time had no interest at all in the sciences but something about this award made me consider it. Afterall I had a piece of paper that said I was good at it. As silly and somewhat conceited as this moment was, it was the first time my life shifted towards research. Some picture evidence of this is shown to the left (I think you can still see the pain from losing math in my eyes a bit).

In middle school, I fell into science. Crash course chemistry, biology, and physics became like comfort shows to me and I could receite Hank Green's lines in those youtube videos. There was something addicting to me about learning how the world worked and the further into it I dove the more I realized I knew nothing and the more I wanted to know. I am happy to say that this passion continued through high school where I pursued the International Baccalaureate program to have access to the higest level of math and science curriculum available. While I learned about my love for science in elementary school, it was in this IB program where I first discovered the power of our immune systems and the devestation that they defend us from.

The University of Florida

I graduated high school fully intent on using this love for science to study medicine and become a doctor. To reach this end I decided to attend the University of Florida (GO GATORS!) and be pre-med. Before my even my first class at UF, I stepped into a research lab as an undergraduate researcher and immediately knew that this was the career I wanted to pursue. Quickly the lab became a primary focus at UF and I worked in three of them over the 3 years that I was there. This allowed me to build up a variety of skills and to see how science can be applied in fields from autoimmune disorders, agriculture, pest control, and genetic disease. The more I learned from my time in the lab the more confident I became in my desire to be a researcher. It was the perfect place for me, it turned the passion for learning and discovery that fueled nights of crash course videos in middle school into a paying career. Being at such a large university also gave me access to explore such a wide variety of fields both in reserach and through classes. I began pusuing classes that would shape me as a scientist. This included coding classes, machine learning, cancer biology, epigenetics, and anything else that would open my eyes to different areas and tools to be used in scientific research.

Emory University

After 3 short years at UF, I graduated and was given a dream opportunity to continue my studies at Emory University as a part of their Cancer Biology Ph.D. program. In my first year, I quickly learned that the cancer and immunology that I knew and understood dwindled in the face of what I was going to learn. The first 2 semesters of classes opened my eyes to research fields I didn't know existed and I could truly feel myself on the cutting edge of science.

With the first year also came rotations. I was given the chance to rotate in three labs where I studied cancer immunology, immunometabolism, and the epigenetic regulation of breast cancer markers. I am lucky to say that these three lab experiences were all incredible each teaching me new skills but in the end I decided to continue my graduate studies by applying my interests in immunology to myeloma through research with Dr. Lawrence Boise.

And this brings us up to now, as a second year graduate student I have taken a project and am continuing to develop new skills and make new connections in and out of my lab. If you want to hear more about the projects I am working on or have worked on in the past please click on my research tab and give me the chance to tell you all about the research that we are doing. Alternatively, keep reading below to hear who I am when I am not talking about science.

There's a Whole World Outside of Lab??

Being a PhD student - while undoubtedly my "calling" - is still quite difficult sometimes. This is why I am so fortunate to have both so many friends and so many hobbies that keep me going in and out of the lab.

From as early as I found my love for science, I have always had a similar passion for sports. I played baseball and soccer from an early age and will play just about anything as long as I have people to play with. In college, I began playing volleyball as well and with it found a group of friends that have been a huge part of my life even today. This love for volleyball and sports more generally has kept with me even today. I am a member of Emory's club volleyball team and am proud to say that I have won intramural volleyball indoor and beach with a group of graduate students in my first year here (although I was advised to not put this award on my CV/Biosketch sadly).

When not playing sports, I can be found having game nights with friends (I am always happy to take a board game suggestion) where I play some of my favorite games like codenames, fishbowl, and more! Also I spend a lot of time taking on various projects. With learning, creating has always been a passion of mine and it has led me to chase hobbies such as coding (which I used to make this website from scratch), blogging (which you can find some of on here), and art as well which I have used to decorate my new apartment in Atlanta.
